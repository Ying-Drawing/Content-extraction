## Development of a processing route for Ti-6Al-4V forgings based on preforms made by selective laser melting

Markus Bambach, Irina Sizova ⁎ , Aliakbar Emdadi

Chair of Mechanical Design and Manufacturing, Brandenburg University of Technology, Cottbus - Senftenberg, Konrad - Wachsmann - Allee 17, Cottbus, D-03046, Germany

## A R T I C L E I N F O

Keywords: Additive manufacturing hot forging microstructure hot working behavior

## 1. Introduction

Due to its high speci fi c strength and good corrosion resistance, the titanium alloy Ti -6Al -4 V is widely used e.g. in the aerospace industry and for medical implants. Parts made of Ti -6Al -4 V are preferably produced by forging in the α + β phase region. As examples in the review by Dutta and Froes [1] prove, the material utilization is often less than 10%. Due to the complex production and high costs of titanium alloys, the low material yield will not be tolerable in the future, since the application of titanium increases. The production cost of Ti -6Al -4 V parts are divided equally (1) into the production of titanium blocks by ore treatment, extraction by means of the Kroll process, alloying and melting in a vacuum arc furnace, and (2) the downstream processing by thermo-mechanical treatment in multistage forging processes [2]. To reduce the cost of titanium production, alternatives to the Kroll process are explored, including electrode oxidation [3] and the preparation of synthetic rutile insert material [4]. All alternative processes produce titanium powder could hence lead to substitution or supplementation of conventional process routes with powder metallurgical (PM) routes in the future. ## A B S T R A C T

Additive manufacturing (AM) provides an alternative for small scale production of various metal components. However, due to the inherent porosity, the performance of AM-parts will not reach the level of forged material unless further processes such as hot-isostatic pressing are used. Alternatively, additive manufacturing could be combined with forging operations to novel process chains, o ff ering the possibility to reduce the number of forging steps and to create near net-shape forgings with desired local properties. In the present study, the design of a novel processing route using additively manufactured preforms for hot working is considered. The microstructure evolution of AM pre-forms is investigated during hot working and heat treatment and compared to conventional wrought material with a lamellar microstructure typically used for forgings. The results show that pre-forms made from Ti-6Al-4 V by SLM show a good hot workability due to the martensitic microstructure. Void closure is observed to occur in hot compression under compressive stresses. As a consequence, the proposed processing route appears to allow for similar fi nal microstructures and hence properties as conventional forging process chains, but may reduce the number of forging steps to a single one and hence reduce the number of dies as well as the die loads.

With regard to resource e ffi ciency, AM processes compete with low material utilization in conventional production. According to Allwood and Cullen, [7] conventional manufacturing routes are more energy e ffi cient than AM for machining rates of up to 75% for steel and only up to 20% for titanium. Due to the low material utilization of the forging process, AM could enable a resource-e ffi cient and possibly more cost-e ff ective production.

The production of parts from the powder bed is typically accomplished by selective laser melting (SLM). SLM was invented only about 20 years ago at the Fraunhofer Institute for Laser Technology in Aachen, Germany (Patent DE 19649865), and has evolved very rapidly since then. The SLM process uses a laser beam to melt metal powder which is spread out in thin layers. Parts are thus created by layerwise melting of powders with a particle size in the range of 10-45 μ m. The high cooling rates of the SLM process lead to the formation of α '-martensite. The SLM process uses a fi nely focused laser beam and achieves a better accuracy than other powder-bed processes such as electron beam melting. A summary of various investigations on SLM in ref. as-built sample conventionally produced Ti -6Al -4 V (elongation &gt; 15%). In combination with process-induced residual stresses, this can lead to the formation of macrocracks (with crack lengths of up to several centimeters) in the production of large-volume and complex components in the SLM process. Over [9] showed that by processing Ti -6Al -4 V with SLM using a substrate plate pre-heated to 500 °C, the process-induced residual stresses, the resulting distortion of a test geometry and macrocracking could be signi fi cantly reduced. Investigations of Xu et al. [10] showed that repeated exposure of the already melted and solidi fi ed surface with a defocused laser beam allows for in-situ heat treatment in the SLM process. This leads to a decomposition of the α '-martensite into the α -and β -phase and thereby to a signi fi cant increase in ductility to about 11%. Zhang and Attar [11] reported, that the SLM-produced samples show higher yield and ultimate strengths in comparison with the cast counterparts, which can be ascribed to the martensitic microstructure. Also the corrosion behavior of Ti-6Al-4 V prepared by SLM was studied in ref. [12,13]. The authors show that the SLM samples possess poorer corrosion resistance than the conventionally produced samples and related this result to the presence of the acicular α ' martensite. Further investigations of Leuders et al. [14] show that the fatigue strength of Ti -6Al -4 V is essentially reduced by binding errors in the volume and near-surface defects. For critical parts, hot isostatic pressing (HIP) is necessary to reduce the porosity in the as-built material. The HIP process removes the columnar microstructures that are generated in AM and creates an equiaxed microstructure [15]. The investigations so far show that targeted process control (for example, preheating, multiple exposure) and removal of bulk defects and residual stresses by HIP may make the resulting mechanical properties of Ti -6Al -4 V comparable to that of forged material.

However, a well-controlled forging process induces a grain fl ow pattern which strengthens the component under fatigue loading. The forged and heat treated material usually shows a fi ne duplex microstructure consisting of the primary α -phase and Widmanstätten ( α + β )-regions. This microstructure is most usually applied and combines the bene fi ts of lamellar and globular microstructures. To reach such a fi nal microstructure and to globularize the α -phase, a thermomechanical processing is required. The strain level applied during forging and the annealing temperature are the dominant factors determining the static globularization kinetics and the fi nal microstructure.

Due to the speci fi c advantages and disadvantages of AM and metal forming processes, the combination of both processes could be benefi cial. If adapted pre-forms could be produced for forging, the forging process could be carried out in a single stage. Currently there are very few studies on the forming behavior of AM titanium, including own preliminary work on the combination of SLM and hot forming [16] and work on laser metal deposition [17]. The main aim of the present study is to close the knowledge gaps regarding the viability of process chains combining AM, hot forming and a fi nishing heat treatment. For this purpose the microstructure evolution of Ti -6Al -4 V pre-forms produced by SLM is investigated during hot working and heat treatment and compared to conventional wrought material with a lamellar microstructure typical for forging. Key questions related to the design of the novel process chain are considered and optimized processing parameters are identi fi ed. The e ff ect of the anisotropy of plastic fl ow of SLM material is examined. The e ffi ciency of forging in closing residual porosity is investigated.

## 2.2. Heat treatment

To investigate the in fl uence of heat treatment on the forming behavior, compression specimens were tested in two di ff erent conditions: (1) SLM+heat treatment and (2) SLM as-built. The heat treatment applied to the SLM specimens consisted of solution heat treatment in a furnace at 955 °C for 0.5 hours followed by air cooling.

To investigate the heat treatment after hot working, two di ff erent conditions were applied:

(i) recrystallization heat treatment: 925 °C for 4 h, cooling at 50 °C/ h to 760 °C followed by air cooling.

(ii) duplex annealing consisting of solution heat treatment 50-75 °C below the β -transus temperature T β for 4 h followed by air cooling [18].

The fi nal operations of ageing heat treatment and machining, which are fi nishing operations necessary for industrial forgings, are not considered.

## 2.3. Hot compression testing

Cylindrical samples (Ø5 mm x 8 mm) were machined from the asbuilt cubes with the cylinder axis parallel, perpendicular and at 45° to the build direction. The cylindrical samples were isothermally deformed at temperatures ranging from 850 °C to 1000 °C, and at strain rates from 0.001 s -1 to 1 s -1 to a total true strain of ∼ 0.9 using a quenching and deformation dilatometer DIL805 A/D/T. The samples were heated by induction-heating under argon gas atmosphere with a heating rate of 10 K/s and held for 3 min. to obtain a homogeneous microstructure before compression testing. Si3N4-punches were used in the compression process. The samples were compressed between molybdenum plates of ∼ 1 mm thickness, which are also heated inductively, to ensure nearly isothermal conditions.

## 2.4. Microstructure analysis

The microstructure of cross sections parallel to the compression direction of the deformed specimens was analyzed by means of optical metallography. Each specimen was quenched immediately after compression at a cooling rate of 200 K/s. The samples were mounted with cut cross-sections on top and ground fl at with successively fi ner grades of silicon carbide (SiC) papers. Then the sample surface was polished with a 0.05 μ mOP-S silica solution. Kroll ' s etching agent was used to reveal the microstructure.

## 2.5. X-ray micro computed tomography analysis

One sample was subjected to X-ray micro computed tomography (microCT), before and after compression testing at 850 °C and strain rate of 0.001 s -1 . The CT scans were performed at a resolution of about 4 μ m. The scans before and after compression were made under identical conditions to ensure that a direct comparison is feasible. The μ CT scans were done with a phoenix nanotom ® m system at a gun voltage of 150 kV and a current of 360 μ A, using 500 ms per image, with 1000 images in one full rotation. All reported analyses were carried out with volume graphics VG StudioMax 3.1 including the defect analysis module. To distinguish between material and pores the grey value threshold was de fi ned. The maximal threshold at which a gray value is interpreted as pores was de fi ned as 8083.28 with a deviation factor of -1.00.

## 3. Results and discussion

## 3.1. Influence of initial heat treatment on microstructure and flow stress behavior

As mentioned above the microstructure of as-built SLM material consists of a fi ne acicular α ' -phase within prior β -grains that are mostly aligned along the building direction (cf. Fig. 2a). This microstructure and its inhomogeneity is the result of the inhomogeneous fast heating and extremely high cooling rate in the SLM process.

After the initial heat treatment (955 °C / 0.5 h / AC) the microstructure consists of a fi ne ' basket weave ' microstructure with an α lath thickness of about 1.6 μ m. In addition, the grain boundary α -phase is observed as well, which is observed as a thin layer on the prior β -grain boundaries (s. Fig. 2b). Dai et al. [19] also researched the heat treatment of SLM-produced Ti-6Al-4 V at di ff erent temperatures and reported similar results, i.e., that the plate-shaped α phase is continuously generated if the heat-treatment temperature is below the β -transus temperature.

The microstructure of conventionally wrought material which is typically used in forging processes is presented in Fig. 2c. It consists of a lamellar alpha phase with an α lath thickness of about 2.6 μ m. The α -phase is also present as a thin layer on the prior β -grain boundaries.

In Fig. 3 the fl ow stresses for selected deformed samples are presented. It was observed, that the as-built material with acicular martensite microstructure shows a lower level of fl ow stresses at all deformation temperatures. The di ff erences in fl ow stress values are pronounced most notably at lowest temperatures, where the deformation is performed in the ( α + β )-two-phase fi eld. At temperatures of 1000 °C or above, all samples show a very similar hot working behavior, but forging in the single β -phase is unfavourable for single hit forming operations.

The micrographs of the samples compressed at a temperature of 850 °C and a strain rate of 0.01 s -1 of directly as-built SLM, heat treated SLM and conventional wrought materials are presented in Fig. 4 a, b, c, respectively. It can be seen that the globularization kinetics of as-built material is faster than the one of heat treated as-built and conventional wrought samples. After compression to the total strain of 0.9 the SLM material shows a fi ne fully globular α -phase in the entire micrograph, whereas the heat treated SLM and conventional wrought materials show high fractions of non-globularized α -phase in the form of segmented platelets.

## 3.2. Hot deformation behavior and microstructure evolution of SLM material

In Fig. 5 the fl ow curves of as-built SLM and conventional wrought specimens are presented at di ff erent temperatures and strain rates. The as-built SLM material shows the lowest values of the peak stresses as well as of the steady-state stresses at the lowest deformation temperatures. However, it is known from the literature that the fl ow stress decreases with increasing of α -lath thickness as a result of the Hall --Petch e ff ect [20]. This e ff ect is most relevant for lower temperatures (850 °C and 900 °C) where the α -phase fraction is larger. Semiatin and Bieler [20] analyzed the hot working behavior of specimens with martensitic and lamellar microstructures and found larger peak stress values in material with a martensitic structure, which is in contrast to the fl ow curves shown in Fig. 5. Perumal et al. [21] also characterized the Ti-6Al-4 V alloy with an acicular microstructure and reported a higher value of fl ow stresses in comparison to the present work. The compression tests in the present study were performed at a much shorter dwell time of 3 min. compared to 10 min. in Ref. [20] and 30 min. in Ref. [21]. It may be assumed that the microstructural transformation α ' → α + β is not completed during heating and dwelling for 3 min. and continues during deformation. Thus, this phenomenon could be responsible for the reduction of the peak fl ow stresses of the SLM material. This e ff ect may be exploited by heating the SLM material quickly to deformation temperature prior to forging by induction heating.

As shown in Fig. 5 c, at the higher temperature of 950 °C, both types of materials show similar levels of fl ow stresses. On the one hand, this may be ascribed to the coarsening of the alpha platelets of the as-built SLM material during preheating to a thickness comparable to that in the conventional wrought material, especially at a lower strain rates. On the other hand, at the temperature of 950 °C the in fl uence of α -phase on the deformation behavior is reduced due to the presence of a high amount of β -phase.

The characteristic values (peak and steady-state stresses) of the fl ow curves for the di ff erent sample orientations tested at the di ff erent temperatures and strain rates are summarized in Fig. 6. The fl ow stresses were almost identical in all cases for all sample directions. Thus, the texture associated with the columnar grain structure elongated along the building direction of the SLM samples seems to have a negligible e ff ect on the fl ow behavior. The biggest di ff erences amounted to only ∼ 1.7% and were detected at the lowest tested temperature. Consequently, it was decided to continue the investigation on the samples produced parallel to the build direction.

To understand the di ff erences in the fl ow behavior, the activation energies of hot deformation (Qw, [kJ·mol -1 ]) were calculated from the fl ow curves for both SLM and conventional material. The procedure of the determination of the Qw is presented above in Section 2.3. Based on Eq. (1), the slope of the plots of ln[sinh( ασ )] versus ln ε ˙ and 1/T were plotted for calculating Qw. The respective plots are presented in Fig. 7. A value of 534 kJ·mol -1 was obtained for the activation energy Qw of as-built SLM Ti -6Al -4 V. For conventional wrought material with a lamellar microstructure a signi fi cantly larger value of 668 kJ·mol -1 was calculated. Both values of Qw are much higher than that for self-di ff usion in α -Ti (150 kJ·mol -1 ) [22]. The lower value of Qw of as-built material could be a consequence of the distinct microstructural initial states, phase fractions and morphologies. It could be also a consequence of a higher density of defects in the rapidly solidi fi ed material. Additionally, the chemical composition of as-built SLM and conventional wrought samples slightly di ff ers (cf. Tables 1 and 2). These di ff erences may also contribute to the di ff erences in Qw values. A lower value of Qw may be advantageous for forging, e.g. to reduce the tool loads and to facilitate the hot working process.

The results indicate that the microstructural mechanisms during hot deformation are quite di ff erent in the SLM and conventional wrought materials. The conventional wrought material should be deformed preferably at lower strain rates, while the SLM material could be deformed much faster in comparison to conventional wrought material with a lamellar starting microstructure.

The examination of the fi nal microstructures shows that at all deformation parameters the as-built material shows a fully globularized microstructure with a fi ne α -phase, while the conventional wrought material was not fully converted to an equiaxed microstructure. Fine equiaxed grains were only observed in some parts of the micrograph. Micrographs for the samples deformed at the temperature of 900 °C and di ff erent strain rates are presented in Fig. 9.

The microstructural reasons for irregular globularization were discussed in the studies of the Semiatin et al. [25] and Weiss et al. [26]. It was put forward that the thickness of the platelets could be an essential factor in fl uencing the globularization processes of the α -phase. Semiatin et al. [25] showed that at fi rst during hot forming, kinking of the α -platelets occurs and the kinking frequency is proportional to the imposed strain. If the strain is large enough, shear bands may lead to segmentation of the α -lamellae. Weiss et al. [26] considered the processes of recrystallization in which the α -grains primarily form within

## 3.3. Examination of porosity evolution during compression

μ CT analyses of the samples before and after compression testing show that the compressed samples have a lower level of porosity in comparison to the as-built samples. By compression to a strain of ∼ 0.9, the majority of the initial porosity was eliminated. The void volume in relation to the total volume of the as-built samples amounts to ∼ 0.01 % after SLM and to ∼ 0.0003 % after compression at 850 °C and 0.001 s -1 . Fig. 10 illustrates the pore size distribution. The maximum void size after SLM was 167 μ m and 86 μ m after compression. The void sizes in as-built SLM material are similar to those found in the study of Liu et al. [27]. The total void volume was reduced from 2.02 × 10 7 μ m 3 to 5.27 × 10 5 μ m 3 after deformation. To achieve a fully dense material the total strain should be further increased. At a strain of 0.9, the sample is no longer compressed homogeneously, so that bulging and hence tensile stresses occur. Future developments will show whether it is possible to decrease the porosity of as built SLM material, so that the combination of SLM and hot working leads to tolerable residual porosity. Also, a more comprehensive study of the void closure behavior could be performed, which would require μ CT analysis for hot forming experiments under di ff erent stress states, which is beyond the scope of the present work.

Stefansson et al. [29] also studied the static globularization behavior of the Ti -6Al -4 V alloy with a colony microstructure, which was deformed and annealed at the temperatures of 900 °C and 955 °C. The authors reported that the pre-strain level and annealing temperature are the essential factors determining the static globularization kinetics. It was shown that the static globularization kinetic was the same independent of the temperature of the deformation process. However, the duration of the segmentation of the α -lamellae and edge spheroidization by annealing at 900 °C were approximately 10 times slower than those at 955 °C. In another study, Semiatin et al. The observations of the authors also showed that the globularization of the α -phase is facilitated by prior hot working, especially by the level of the strain.

Depending on the fi nal heat treatment, di ff erent microstructures with the desired properties can be produced. Recrystallization heat treatment is usually used to improve fracture toughness [18]. During this annealing a microstructure with equiaxed α -grains and β -phase at grain boundary triple points is created (s. Fig. 11a). Duplex annealing alters the shapes, sizes, and distributions of phases to those required for improved creep resistance or fracture toughness [30]. During annealing near the β -transus temperature, globularization of the deformed α occurs and a duplex microstructure with primary α -phase and Widmanstätten ( α + β )-regions is produced (s. Fig. 11b).

## 4. Conclusion

The present study addresses questions related to the viability and design of novel processing routes consisting of additive manufacturing of pre-forms, hot forming and annealing. The heat treatment operations, the hot deformation behavior and the microstructure evolution of AM pre-forms were considered and compared to conventional wrought material with a lamellar microstructure typical for forging. The following conclusions are relevant for this work and future research:

- Heat treatment of the as-built material before hot forming is not needed. The as-built SLM material has a good hot workability.

- The fl ow stress behavior as well as the post-forming microstructure of as-built SLM material are very similar to those produced by conventional wrought material. In general the as-built material shows lower levels of fl ow stresses and also a higher rate of dynamic globularization of the α -phase than conventional material. Furthermore, the as-built SLM material can be deformed at higher strain rates in comparison to conventional wrought material with lamellar starting microstructure.
- The as-built material shows a lower level of activation energy of hot working in comparison to conventional wrought material, which may be advantageous for forging processes, e.g. to reduce the tool loads and to facilitate the hot working.
- Hot compression reduces the porosity of the SLM pre-forms.
- Based on the application of the fi nal post-working heat treatment following the hot forming process, di ff erent types of desired microstructures can be produced, similar to conventional wrought material.
